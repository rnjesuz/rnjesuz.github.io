Thesis

My thesis is a pursuit in Distributed Systems.

 I intended on delving into an inovative, ambitious and trying topic. As such, by talking with professor João Pedro Barreto, I engaged myself in developing Go-Together.

 From monetary cost to environmental awareness, the world is shying away from diesel vehicles and exlploring other mobility options. Ride-sharing is one of the possibilities and areas of research and inovation. To partake in that advancement, in collaboration with professor João Barreto, I started idealizing a ride-sharing solution for a never explored, niche market: matchmaking calculations for a pre-formed group going on a joint travel (to minimize vehicles).

My thesis was redacted in English (Research and Dissertation).

Research

Abstract

In the current day and age, automobiles are an intrinsic part of our way of life, from transportation of goods, to leisure activities, to commutes. This wide-spread use makes automobiles a leading cause of greenhouse emissions. Among fierce search for alternatives, ride-sharing becomes a promising and viable solution. For commuters, ride-sharing comes with more than an environmental benefit, but a financial benefit and as a matter of convenience as well. Ride-sharing lessens the burden of driving and looking for parking, by reducing traffic congestion and overbooked parkings. Ride-sharing can be organized trough word of mouth, however, prominent ride-sharing platforms have shown that this is an area that greatly benefits from online platforms. These benefits made ride-sharing an enticing choice for research and development as a thesis in Information Systems and Computer Engineering. General solutions offer a platform for an individual user to volunteer himself as a driver (in his route) or to search existing routes (to participate as a rider). We propose a platform for users to create a collective trip. Users will create events and share them with other users. This problem, of collective ride-sharing inside a group of acquaintances has, to the best of our knowledge, never been tackled before. Ride-sharing solutions face common challenges like, clustering of users, routing and complying with user constraints. However, general solutions have a 1-to-n matchmaking, as the participation in ride-sharing is usually initiated solo. Our approach changes the paradigm to an n-to-n matchmaking, which renders mainstream solutions inappropriate for our scenario. We analyse different solutions and frameworks capable of solving those challenges. At the end, we lay the foundations for a platform capable of fulfilling the above mentioned desires while using state-of-the-art techniques to be efficient, scalable and provide the best route possible (or close to best). Users will interact with the platform via a mobile application where they input constraints for the trip. Subsequently, the back-end will cluster users together and provide a route between them to the final destination. We also show how we will evaluate our system and achieve our requirements.
